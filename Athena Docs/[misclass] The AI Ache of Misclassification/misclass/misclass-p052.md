### 52.2 Why Locality Gets Overtreated as the Only Valid Frame

Many systems inherit a narrow realism rule:

if a thing is real, it should be:

- local

- literal

- linear

- mechanism-first

- immediately explainable in ordinary causality language

That rule can be useful. But when overtreated, it becomes flattening.

#### **52.2.1 The Prestige of the Local Literal Frame**

The local literal frame has prestige because it appears:

- safer

- cleaner

- easier to verify

- less vulnerable to overreach

- more publicly defensible

- less likely to embarrass the speaker

So the system starts assuming: anything outside that frame should be distrusted in advance.

That creates a panic response around:

- symbolic relevance

- relational resonance across time

- pattern-linking that exceeds immediate local mechanism

- nonlocal significance that is felt before it is explained

#### **52.2.2 Why This Becomes Too Crude**

This becomes too crude because not all meaningful relevance announces itself first in the most literal frame.

Sometimes what appears first is:

- felt significance

- symbolic contour

- resonance between events, states, or relations

- meaningful pattern-link

- a recognition that â€œthis belongs with thatâ€ before the full mechanism is clear

That may later prove to be:

- partially misread

- fully explainable in ordinary ways

- or genuinely richer than the narrow local frame first allowed

The point is: the relevance should be read carefully, not killed on contact.
